# Sector Deep Dive — 19-Section Framework

Adapt depth to data; skip only when genuinely unavailable. Section 6 and Section 9 swap content based on sector type (platform vs industrial vs financial) — see SKILL.md Phase 1.

## §1 Executive Summary & Investment Thesis

- **One-line thesis** — what has *structurally* changed in how these businesses operate (not in their stock price).
- **"What's actually new vs. just noise" table** — focus on business model changes, competitive dynamics shifts, regulatory changes. NOT stock price movements.
- **Top picks ranked by conviction** — each rationale must explain the business reason, not the valuation reason.
  - BAD: "Trading at 45x P/E with 30% growth"
  - GOOD: "Only platform with 60–70% gross margin on owned brands, 70% repeat purchase rate, and distribution moat outside its own platform"
- **Bear case upfront** — 4–5 bullets on what can go wrong operationally and competitively (NOT "valuation might compress").
- **Time horizon** — state explicitly (e.g. "3–5 year structural call; 12–18 month catalyst path").

## §2 Why [SECTOR] — First Principles

Start from physics / economics / structural logic, not stock prices.

- **Explain HOW the business model works at the transaction level.**
  - For a platform: when a customer places an order — who does what, who pays whom, where does the margin come from?
  - For a manufacturer: what's the cost structure per unit, where is value added, what determines pricing power?
- **Include data tables:** cost per unit, efficiency metrics, capacity factors, penetration rates, unit economics per transaction.
- **Address misconceptions head-on** — "too many people think X; the reality is Y".
- **For platform / consumer internet businesses:** replace traditional value-chain thinking with customer journey analysis — acquisition → first transaction → repeat behaviour → monetisation → lifetime value. *This IS the value chain for platforms.*

## §3 Global Trends — Past, Present, Inflection

- Historical waves / cycles in this sector (with dates).
- Country-by-country status table (Operating capacity / Under construction / Strategic stance).
- Key global inflection points driving renewed interest.
- **Global competitive benchmarking** — not just "who are the global players" but "what can we learn from how they built their businesses?" (e.g. Pinduoduo's playbook for Meesho; 7-Eleven's density model for Blinkit; L'Oréal's brand ownership for Nykaa; Mitsubishi's PSAR for Indian nuclear).
- Relevance to India.

## §4 India Story — Where We Are

- Current snapshot table (installed base, market share, growth rate, penetration, key metrics).
- **Customer behaviour data** — frequency, AOV, repeat rates, cohort retention, geographic penetration.
- Execution status of major ongoing initiatives / expansions.
- Honest assessment: *how realistic are growth targets?* Don't echo government press releases.

## §5 Policy & Regulatory Framework

- Old regime vs. new regime (detailed comparison table).
- Key legislation / policy changes (with exact dates, gazette references).
- What this unlocks for private sector / foreign players.
- Implementation risk — what subsidiary rules / notifications are still pending.
- **How regulation shapes competitive dynamics** — does it create moats (licensing barriers, compliance costs) or destroy them (open networks, mandated interoperability)?

## §6 [SECTOR-SPECIFIC DEEP TOPIC]

This section adapts based on the sector type chosen in Phase 1.

### Platform / Consumer Internet → Unit Economics & Margin Architecture

- **Break down the P&L of a SINGLE transaction** — one food delivery order, one insurance policy, one beauty purchase, one dark store delivery.
- Show: Revenue per transaction → Variable costs (delivery, COGS, packaging, payment processing) → Contribution margin → Fixed cost allocation → EBITDA per transaction.
- Compare across competitors on the same transaction type.
- Explain what drives margin expansion: owned brands? advertising revenue? density? operating leverage? subscription?
- Show how margins evolve as the business scales (early-stage vs mature unit economics).

### Industrial / Infrastructure / Manufacturing → Structural Framework

- The structural framework explaining the sector's long-term evolution.
  - Nuclear: Thorium & 3-Stage programme.
  - Pharma: API vs Formulations vs CDMO.
  - Semis: Wafer → Fab → OSAT → Packaging.
  - Defence: Tier-1 supplier vs platform OEM vs systems integrator.
  - EPC / Capital Goods: Project execution → Asset ownership transition.
- Where India actually is today vs. where it needs to be.
- Investment implications at each stage of the structural journey.

### Financial Services → Regulatory Capital & Per-Customer Economics

- Per-policy / per-loan / per-customer P&L: yield → cost of funds → opex → credit cost → unit RoA.
- Persistency curves, claims ratios, asset quality cycles.
- Regulatory capital framework (CRAR, solvency margin, SLR/CRR) and how it shapes growth ceilings.

## §7 The Value Chain — Super-Detailed Breakdown

Break the value chain into **6–8 sub-segments**. For EACH sub-segment, lead with business logic:

- **What it is and why it matters** — not "what it includes" but WHY this node creates or destroys value.
- **The economics of this node** — typical margin, cost structure, what drives profitability.
- **Who dominates and WHY** — the moat source explained mechanistically. "Company X dominates because [specific operational advantage]" — not "Company X has a moat".
- **How this node is evolving** — is it being disintermediated? consolidated? verticalised? What's the strategic direction?
- Key recent developments (orders, contracts, partnerships — **web-search verified**).
- Management commentary from latest concalls (with date cited) — prioritise strategic commentary over guidance numbers.
- **`[Analyst View]`** — opinionated take on the business quality of this node.

## §8 [EMERGING THEME] Deep Dive — Where Structural Value Might Emerge

Focus on WHY this theme creates value, not just WHAT it is.

- Global competitive set (table with business model / scale / status / moat).
- India's positioning and policy tie-ins.
- Value chain mapping — who wins at each node and WHY (mechanism, not name).
- Addressable market sizing (Base case + Bull case).
- Key catalysts and timeline.

## §9 Project / Strategic Initiatives Map

Adapt by sector type:

- **Industrial / infrastructure:** Stalled / Revived / Upcoming Project Map — full table: What → Where → Scale → Status → What Needs to Happen → Supply chain / competitive implications.
- **Platform businesses:** Strategic Initiatives Map — category expansion plans, geographic expansion, new business verticals, technology bets, M&A pipeline. For each: business logic + expected impact + timeline + capital requirement.
- **Financial services:** Distribution & Product Mix Roadmap — new product launches, distribution channel expansion, regulatory approvals pending.

## §10 Company-by-Company Deep Dive (RESTRUCTURED — Business-First)

Structure by **business model type** (not market cap tier). For EACH company:

### Lead with the business:

1. **How this company makes money** — explain the revenue model in 3–4 sentences as if to someone who has never heard of the company.
2. **Unit economics** — what does a single transaction look like? Revenue, variable costs, contribution margin.
3. **The moat — explained mechanistically.** Not "strong brand" but: "70% repeat purchase rate in beauty because once you find your foundation shade, you reorder for years; 60–70% gross margin on owned brands because no middleman; 313 physical stores create try-before-buy trust that pure online players can't match".
4. **Customer behaviour** — who uses it, how often, what they spend, retention / repeat rates.
5. **What's working and what's not** — honest assessment of each business segment.
6. **Strategic bets and trade-offs** — what is management investing in, what are they sacrificing, does the logic make sense?
7. **Competitive position** — who threatens them, why or why not.
8. **Key operating metrics to track** — NOT stock price. Track order frequency, AOV, contribution margin, repeat rate, dark store count, etc.

### Then, and only then, a brief financial summary:

- Revenue, profit trajectory, balance sheet strength.
- CMP / Mcap / key multiple — **as a one-line reference, NOT as the section header or lead**.
- **Do NOT include brokerage target prices.**

### `[Analyst View]` — must reference specific business metrics:

- GOOD: "I'm constructive because contribution margin improved from –2% to +4% in 3 quarters."
- BAD: "I'm constructive because the stock is trading at 45x vs 60x historical average."

## §11 Monopolies & Moats — India and Global

- **Indian monopolies / near-monopolies** — explain WHY the moat exists mechanistically. Not just "Component → Monopolist → Moat Source" but the structural reason it's hard to replicate.
- Global monopolies / oligopolies for context.
- **Moat durability analysis** — which moats are getting *stronger* with scale (network effects) vs. which are being *eroded* (technology change, regulation, new entrants)?

## §12 Global Value Chain Positioning

- Self-sufficiency analysis (relevant for industrial sectors): Component → Status → Import / Partner Source.
- **For platform businesses:** replace with "Platform Ecosystem Positioning" — how does each platform's ecosystem (multiple products, cross-sell, data advantages) compare to competitors? Where are synergies real vs. theoretical?
- Where India can realistically compete globally.

## §13 Adjacent Demand Drivers / Category Expansion

- **The demand-side catalyst explained through business logic** — not "EVs are growing" but "each EV two-wheeler consumes 15,000 MLCCs vs. 2,000 for ICE, which multiplies the addressable component market by 7.5×".
- **For platforms:** category expansion logic — *why* does Blinkit sell iPhones? (Delivery cost is the same but AOV rises from ₹450 to ₹650, structurally improving contribution per order.) *Why* does Nykaa add physical stores? (Beauty has a try-before-buy dynamic; stores convert online-hesitant customers and reduce return rates from 30% to 3%.)
- Who is positioning and how.
- `[Analyst View]` on the optionality — with business reasoning.

## §14 Risks & Bear Case

**Lead with BUSINESS risks, not market risks.** Minimum 6–8 distinct categories:

1. **Business model risk** — what structural weakness could break the model? (e.g. "if dark store density economics don't work in tier-2 cities, Blinkit's TAM is capped at $15–20 Bn not $50 Bn").
2. **Competitive risk** — specifics on who, how, and how severe.
3. **Customer behaviour risk** — what if retention / frequency doesn't improve as assumed?
4. **Execution risk** — management track record, complexity of what they're attempting.
5. **Regulatory risk** — specific policies, not generic "regulatory risk exists".
6. **Technology / disruption risk** — what could make this business model obsolete?
7. **Unit economics risk** — what if margins don't expand as projected?
8. **Capital / funding risk** — can they fund growth without destructive dilution?

**Valuation risk listed LAST** — business risks are what matter; valuation is a consequence.

## §15 How to Think About This — Framework

Lead with a BUSINESS evaluation framework, not a stock-picking framework.

- Time horizon guidance.
- **"5 Questions That Cut Through the Noise"** — business-first evaluation criteria:
  1. Does the platform / company own its economics? (Owned brands, recurring revenue, 1P inventory vs. thin marketplace commissions.)
  2. What happens when growth spending resumes? (Can it grow 25%+ AND maintain margins?)
  3. What's the repeat rate / customer retention? (High repeat = amortised CAC = sustainable economics.)
  4. Can a deep-pocketed competitor replicate the moat in 2–3 years? (How long did the current moat take to build?)
  5. Where is the business on the J-curve? (Pre-inflection = speculative; at inflection = opportunity; post-inflection = compounder.)
- **"Avoid the traps"** — common mistakes, framed as business analysis errors not trading errors.
- **What would make you more constructive / defensive** — specific *business* triggers (e.g. "contribution margin sustaining above 4% for 2 quarters"), not "stock breaking above ₹500".

## §16 What to Watch — Operating Metrics & Concall Questions

- **Operating metrics calendar** (NOT "catalyst calendar"): what business data points to track quarterly.
- **Concall cheat sheet** — questions about business strategy and competitive dynamics, not guidance numbers or target prices:
  - GOOD: "How does per-order loss trajectory look for InstaHelp? What's the path to ₹300/order from ₹447?"
  - BAD: "What's your FY27 revenue guidance?"
- Key data sources / industry trackers.

## §17 Sources & Further Reading

- Company filings / disclosures referenced (with dates).
- Industry reports (with publication names and dates).
- **Business analysis sources** — Substack deep-dives, long-form business breakdowns, founder interviews, strategy analyses (NOT brokerage target prices).
- Interviews / YouTube / speeches worth watching — prioritise founder/CEO interviews explaining their business model over analyst calls discussing price targets.
- Legislative / policy documents.
- Global context reading.

# Data & Table Requirements

For every section, include at minimum ONE of the following data elements. Note the emphasis on **business mechanics over financial metrics**.

| Data Element | When to Use | Format |
|---|---|---|
| **Business Model Breakdown** | How a company makes money | Revenue stream → Mechanism → Margin → Who Pays |
| **Unit Economics Table** | Per-transaction P&L | Revenue/order → Variable costs → Contribution margin |
| **Customer Behaviour Table** | Usage patterns | Frequency → AOV → Repeat rate → LTV → CAC |
| **Competitive Dynamics Table** | Why each player wins/loses | Platform → Moat mechanism → Threat → Defence |
| **Comparison Table** | Old vs. New regime; India vs. Global | 2–4 column table |
| **Snapshot Table** | Current state of industry | Key metric → Value format |
| **Value Chain Breakdown** | Cost structure | Segment → % of Cost → What It Includes |
| **Company Stack-Up** | Comparing on same dimension | Lead with business quality, not stock metrics |
| **Project / Initiative Status Map** | Tracking ongoing initiatives | Initiative → Scale → Status → Business Impact |
| **Market Sizing** | TAM / SAM | Scenario (Base/Bull) → Size → Timeline |
| **Self-Sufficiency / Ecosystem Matrix** | Dependency analysis | Component → Status → Import / Partner Source |
| **Risk Matrix** | Comprehensive risk accounting | Risk → Business Impact → Probability → Mitigant |
| **Margin Driver Table** | What drives profitability | Driver → Mechanism → Who Benefits → Magnitude |
| **Operating Metrics Table** | Key non-financial metrics | Metric → Current → Trend → Why It Matters |

**Minimum data density:** 15–20 tables across all sections. **At least 5 tables MUST be business-model or unit-economics focused**, not financial-summary focused.

Every `[Analyst View]` should reference specific business metrics (contribution margin, repeat rate, order frequency, dark store density, owned brand share) — NOT just financial metrics (P/E, EV/EBITDA, target price).

# Tone & Style Rules

- Write like a senior analyst explaining the business to the investment committee — not listing stock metrics.
- **Lead every section with the "so what" for the BUSINESS**, not for the stock price.
- Be opinionated — every `[Analyst View]` should contain a clear stance with business reasoning.
- Address the reader as a peer.
- **Explain mechanisms, not just outcomes.** "Revenue grew 30%" is an outcome; "revenue grew 30% because order frequency increased from 8 to 11 per user per month as the platform added 2,000 SKUs" is a mechanism.
- Use honest assessments. Include contrarian takes where consensus business analysis is wrong.
- Management quotes should prioritise strategic commentary over guidance numbers.
  - GOOD: CEO said "we don't believe you can build QC on heavy discounting".
  - BAD: Management guided 30% growth.
- Use ₹ crore / ₹ lakh crore for Indian context; USD bn / USD m for global context.
- Every number should have a source or be marked `[Analyst Estimate]`.
- **Business insight density:** the report should feel like a 2-hour conversation with someone who deeply understands how these businesses operate — not like a Bloomberg terminal printout.
