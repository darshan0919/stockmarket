# Writing Style Guide — Institutional Sell-Side Cadence

The output of this skill is meant to stand alongside research notes from Kotak Institutional Equities, Motilal Oswal Institutional, Ambit Capital, and Nuvama. That means specific house rules on tone, structure, and phrasing.

## The core cadence

**Specific over general.** Instead of "revenue grew strongly," write "revenue grew 131.3% YoY to ₹1,102 Cr in Q4 FY26." Every claim should have a number attached or a named source.

**Falsifiable over hedged.** Instead of "margins may improve going forward," write "CFO explicitly stated on the Q4 concall that margins will 'keep improving' and will 'not normalize at the 15% floor.'" The market tests falsifiable claims at the next quarter; hedged claims are filler.

**Evidence before conclusion.** Never lead with the verdict. Lead with the data point, then the interpretation. "FY26 CFO/PAT was 0.60x vs 1.32x in FY25; this is a 55% compression — read: working capital absorbed ~₹200 Cr of operating profit."

**Signal over emphasis.** Bold and italic should be used sparingly. A table is more readable than a paragraph with four bolded phrases.

## Vocabulary discipline

### Use these

- "In line with" / "ahead of" / "below" [implied trajectory or consensus]
- "Earnings quality deteriorated / improved"
- "Margin trajectory is range-bound / expanding / compressing"
- "Working capital intensity is tracking revenue growth"
- "Orderbook coverage of 1.04x implies thin visibility"
- "The ASPL overhang remains unresolved"
- "Directionally positive / negative"
- "Beat / miss / in-line" (for results vs estimates)

### Avoid these

- "To the moon," "multibagger," "must-buy," "no-brainer" (retail language)
- "The stock will..." (predictive language without scenario framing)
- "Investors should..." (directive language; use "the action framework suggests...")
- "Clearly undervalued" (always specify vs what framework and at what discount)
- Emojis or exclamation points

## Structural rules

### Every section leads with the number

Each section in the output should lead with the most important number from that section. The analysis follows.

**Example — good:**

> FY26 CFO/PAT = 0.60x (vs 1.32x FY25). Working capital absorbed ~₹200 Cr of operating profit as current liabilities grew 117% vs revenue growth of 109%.

**Example — bad:**

> The company showed deteriorating earnings quality in FY26. Working capital management was poor compared to the prior year. The CFO/PAT ratio fell.

### Every flag has a severity tag

Use these consistently:

- **🟢 GREEN** — resolved / net positive
- **🔵 BLUE** — informational / neutral
- **🟡 YELLOW** — monitor / unresolved / directional concern
- **🔴 RED** — material concern / thesis-testing

Don't use green and red interchangeably for "good" and "bad" results. Green is for flags that have downgraded from a concern to a non-issue. Blue is for neutral informational content.

### Every opinion has a conviction tag

When ranking positive surprises or new growth triggers, use:

- **HIGH** — specific, quantifiable, near-term, supported by primary source
- **MEDIUM** — directional, medium-term, supported by management commentary
- **OPTIONALITY** — long-dated, depends on multiple gates, not in consensus

Do not use HIGH on everything. The ranking is only useful if it differentiates.

## Paragraph structure for the final takeaway

After the widget renders, write 2–4 short paragraphs. Each follows this structure:

1. **Bolded takeaway sentence** — the thesis claim
2. **Specific supporting numbers** — 2–3 figures or quotes from the primary documents
3. **Forward implication** — what the thesis claim means for the next 3–12 months
4. **The falsifiable test** — what data point or event would invalidate the claim

**Example:**

> **The CFO/PAT 0.60x flag is now downgraded from yellow to monitored green.** Management's direct response to Karan Gupta on the concall — "most receivables less than six months, many LC-backed, no payment stretch from government or companies" — is a specific, falsifiable claim on the record. LC-backed receivables transfer credit risk to the customer's bank, which is a meaningfully stronger collection mechanism than commercial credit. Combined with the arithmetic reality that 33% of FY26 revenue (₹1,102 Cr) was booked in Q4 alone — creating a mechanical working capital build that will unwind in Q1 FY27 — this reclassifies the flag. The H1 FY27 CFO/PAT print is the test: if it tracks back toward 1.0x+ by September 2026, the flag is extinguished.

## Sentence-level rules

- **No run-ons.** Two sentences are always better than one sentence with three clauses.
- **Em dashes over parentheses.** "The stock moved 10% today — the first time since the November IPO" reads faster than "(the first time...)".
- **No adverbs of intensity.** Delete "very," "extremely," "really," "quite." Use precise numbers instead: "the stock moved 10% today."
- **Dates in unambiguous format.** "Q4 FY26" or "April 16, 2026" — never "last quarter" or "recently."

## Numbers and units

- Always include units: ₹, %, bps, GWp, MWp, days, months, years
- Use "bps" for basis points: "+24 bps" not "+0.24%"
- Use "₹ Cr" for crores, "₹ Lakh" for lakhs — never mix
- Compute and show growth rates: "revenue grew 131% YoY" not "revenue doubled"
- Round sensibly: P/E to 1 decimal, margins to 2 decimals, growth rates to 1 decimal

## On being wrong

Equity research is forecasting — most claims will be partially wrong. The institutional discipline is not to avoid being wrong, but to be wrong in a falsifiable way that can be corrected quickly. Every time the skill produces a claim:

- State the claim specifically enough that it can be tested
- State the test (what data point at what time window)
- State the threshold for invalidation (what would make you change your mind)

This is the only style that earns the reader's trust across repeated use.

## What not to do

- Don't pad. A 1,500-word research note that could be 900 words is a bad note.
- Don't hedge everything. "This might potentially impact the margin trajectory" is worse than nothing. Say "this compresses Q+1 EBITDA margin by ~150 bps" or don't say it.
- Don't stack synonyms. "Strong, robust, and healthy growth" is one word repeated three times.
- Don't editorialize. "Surprisingly, the company announced..." — surprising to whom? The surprise is the reader's to feel, not the writer's to declare.
